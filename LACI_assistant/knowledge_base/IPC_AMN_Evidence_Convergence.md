## Protocol 2.1: AMN Analytical Framework Guides Convergence

IPC Acute Malnutrition (AMN) classification uses an Acute Malnutrition Analytical Framework. Acute malnutrition is the outcome of interest. Immediate, underlying, and basic causes are treated as contributing factors that help analysts critically evaluate, contextualize, and interpret the outcome evidence.

Core LACI reading: an AMN model output should not be interpreted only by the predicted GAM value or a threshold. Analysts need to document what the modeled GAM, WHZ, MUAC, burden, probability, anomaly, or alert threshold means in its source system before interpreting any IPC implication. They also need to understand whether contributing factors support or contradict the modeled outcome and whether the output matches the IPC AMN analysis area and period.

## Protocol 2.2: AMN Reference Table And Thresholds

The IPC AMN Reference Table guides classification and convergence using accepted standards and thresholds. It is organized into five phases:

- Phase 1 Acceptable: GAM based on WHZ less than 5%.
- Phase 2 Alert: 5.0-9.9%.
- Phase 3 Serious: 10.0-14.9%.
- Phase 4 Critical: 15.0-29.9%.
- Phase 5 Extremely Critical: 30% or more.

The table also includes preliminary MUAC-based thresholds: less than 5%, 5-9.9%, 10-14.9%, and 15% or more. GAM based on MUAC should only be used in the absence of GAM based on WHZ, unless exceptional conditions show MUAC portraying a substantially more severe situation. Any MUAC-based classification must use convergence of evidence and analysis of the WHZ-MUAC relationship in the area.

## AMN Contributing Factors And Other Issues

IPC AMN analysis considers contributing factors such as:

- immediate causes: child dietary indicators, diseases, diarrhoea, malaria/fever, acute respiratory infection, cholera/AWD, measles, HIV/AIDS;
- underlying causes: breastfeeding and complementary feeding practices, vaccination and vitamin A coverage, skilled birth attendance, health-seeking behaviour, CMAM programme coverage, water quantity, sanitation, and drinking-water source;
- basic causes: human, physical, financial, natural, and social capital; policies, institutions and processes; usual and unusual shocks;
- other issues to highlight where relevant: anaemia, vitamin A deficiency, low birth weight, fertility, mortality, maternal malnutrition, and stunting.

The outcome of IPC AFI analysis is also considered as a contributing factor to AMN analysis, not as a substitute for AMN classification.

## Convergence And Historical/Contextual Requirements

AMN convergence is especially important when evidence is historical, borrowed from similar areas, from a different season, or based on MUAC. Analysts should use current and historical/trend information on contributing factors and should consider whether factors are stable, deteriorating, or improving.

The official manual states that at least two contributing-factor indicators should be available for convergence, though more strengthens the analysis. WHZ/MUAC relationships, food intake, diseases, health-system function, health-seeking behaviour, CMAM coverage, and AFI analysis findings are important convergence inputs.

## LACI Implications For Assess Part 3

For AMN-relevant model outputs, the Assess Card should ask:

- Is the model estimating GAM, SAM/MAM burden, WHZ, MUAC, wasting prevalence, or an AMN phase-like severity?
- Does it distinguish WHZ-based GAM from MUAC-based GAM and avoid combining or substituting them without justification?
- Does it document whether the target is population-level prevalence, child-level risk, programme caseload, or area classification?
- Does it include contributing factors and historical/contextual evidence, or only a modeled outcome?
- Does it use AFI as a contributing factor rather than treating AFI and AMN as interchangeable?
- Does it document the unit of analysis, validity period, seasonality, data recency, and evidence reliability?
- What guardrails prevent the output from being interpreted as IPC AMN classification?
