# IPC Protocol 2A AFI Anchor: Convergence, Reference Table, Thresholds, and LACI Implications

**Status:** confirmed wiki anchor from IPC Manual v3.1, Part 2A Acute Food Insecurity Classification.

## What AFI Classification Is Trying To Answer

IPC Acute Food Insecurity (AFI) Function 2 classifies severity and identifies key drivers. The analysis should answer: how severe the situation is, when populations are or will be acutely food insecure, where they are located, how many people are affected, why they are affected, and who is most affected.

## Protocol 2.1: Analytical Framework Guides Convergence

AFI uses the Food Security Analytical Framework to guide convergence of evidence. The framework separates:

- **Contributing factors:** causal factors, vulnerabilities, hazards or shocks, and the food security dimensions of availability, access, household utilization, and stability.
- **First-level outcomes:** food consumption and livelihood change.
- **Second-level outcomes:** nutritional status and mortality.

Core LACI reading: a model that estimates one driver, one indicator, or one proxy does not by itself reproduce AFI classification. IPC classification depends on convergence across outcome evidence, contributing factors, context, and analyst reasoning.

## Protocol 2.2: Reference Table And Thresholds

The AFI Reference Table guides convergence by using generally accepted international standards and cut-offs. It is organized around five phases: Phase 1 Minimal/None, Phase 2 Stressed, Phase 3 Crisis, Phase 4 Emergency, and Phase 5 Catastrophe/Famine.

The table includes phase descriptions, priority response objectives, common characteristics, outcome indicators, and contributing-factor descriptions. It identifies comparable cut-offs for selected food security outcome indicators so households can be distributed across phases.

Important constraint: the Reference Table provides guidance for evidence alignment, but final classification comes from convergence of evidence, contextualization, understanding of indicator limitations, and documented analyst reasoning. Indicators do not always align.

## Direct And Indirect Evidence

The Reference Table identifies selected direct evidence indicators, but IPC also encourages other relevant evidence. Evidence not listed in the Reference Table, including local outcome indicators and most contributing-factor indicators, is indirect evidence and must be interpreted in context. Locally calibrated cut-offs may be used when available; otherwise analysts infer meaning in relation to phase descriptions and other indicator cut-offs.

## AFI Indicators And Factors To Keep Visible In LACI

For AFI-relevant model outputs, LACI should distinguish:

- food consumption evidence, including energy intake, FCS, HDDS, HHS, rCSI, FIES, and related indicators where applicable;
- livelihood change and coping, including HEA survival/livelihood protection thresholds and livelihood coping strategies;
- second-level outcomes, especially acute malnutrition and mortality, only when food consumption deficits plausibly explain them;
- contributing factors, including hazards, vulnerability, availability, access, utilization, stability, conflict, displacement, markets, prices, assistance, access, disease, and other contextual shocks.

## Threshold Logic Caveat

Thresholds help align evidence but do not mechanically define a phase outside the IPC convergence process. LACI must flag any model that treats a single threshold, modeled score, alert, or predicted phase as equivalent to IPC AFI classification.

## Population Estimates And 20 Percent Rule

AFI area classification considers the most severe phase affecting at least 20 percent of the relevant population or households, interpreted through convergence of evidence. For LACI, model outputs that estimate affected populations, Phase 3+, Phase 4+, or phase-like categories must document the denominator, population vintage, phase/threshold disaggregation, uncertainty, and whether Phase 3+ is separated from Phase 4+ or Phase 5. A Phase 3+ aggregate alone cannot support inference about Phase 4+ or more severe area-phase implications.

Model outputs may inform analyst reasoning about population distribution only as bounded supporting information. They do not become official IPC population estimates or area classifications unless accepted through IPC processes.

## LACI Implications For Assess Part 3

For IPC Outcome Modeling, IPC-Indicative, or proxy-trained AFI outputs, the Assess Card should ask:

- Is the target AFI phase, AFI-compatible severity, FEWS NET/IPC-like labels, affected population, or a contributing factor?
- Does the model distinguish outcome evidence from contributing factors?
- Does it collapse convergence of evidence into one score or threshold?
- Are acute malnutrition or mortality being used only when food consumption deficits plausibly explain them?
- Does the model output risk being read as IPC classification or official population estimates?
- Are geography, unit of analysis, validity period, and evidence reliability documented?
